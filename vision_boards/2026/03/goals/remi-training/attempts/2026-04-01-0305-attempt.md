## Understanding Reinforcement Schedules for Remi's Training

### Current Phase: Continuous Reinforcement
Remi likely started with **continuous reinforcement (CR)** — receiving a treat every time he performed the desired behavior. This is ideal for initial learning but creates dependency and is impractical for long-term public training.

### Target Phase: Intermittent Reinforcement
Moving to **intermittent (partial) reinforcement** will:
- Make behavior more resistant to extinction
- Reduce Remi's dependency on seeing a treat before responding
- Create a more relaxed, confident public demeanor
- Allow for longer café/restaurant sessions without over-treating

### Recommended Schedule Type: Variable Interval (VI)
For Remi's calm-under-the-table behavior, a **variable interval schedule** is ideal:
- Rewards come after unpredictable amounts of time (e.g., 5 seconds, then 12 seconds, then 3 seconds)
- Creates steady, consistent response rates
- Prevents Remi from checking the clock or becoming anxious about when treats come
- Best for maintaining duration-based behaviors like "get under" and stay

## Step-by-Step Interval Extension Protocol

### Phase 1: Establish Baseline (Week 1)
| Current Interval | Target Behavior |
|------------------|-----------------|
| Every 30-60 seconds | Calm, quiet, settled under table |

**Actions:**
1. Track current treat frequency during sessions
2. Note Remi's behavior quality at each interval
3. Identify his "breaking point" — when he starts to get restless

### Phase 2: Gradual Extension (Weeks 2-4)
**Rule:** Extend by **2-3 seconds** only when Remi maintains calm for 90% of the interval.

**Progression Chart:**

| Stage | Interval Between Treats | Session Duration Goal |
|-------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Baseline | 30 seconds | 20 min |
| Stage 1 | 45 seconds | 20 min |
| Stage 2 | 60 seconds | 25 min |
| Stage 3 | 90 seconds | 30 min |
| Stage 4 | 2 minutes | 35 min |
| Stage 5 | 3 minutes | 40 min |
| Stage 6 | 5 minutes | 45 min |

**Important:** Each stage should take **3-5 sessions** before advancing. Only move up when Remi is consistently calm.

### Phase 3: Variable Intervals (Weeks 5+)
Once Remi can hold for 3+ minutes consistently, introduce variability:

**Example Variable Schedule (average: 2 minutes):**
- Wait 30 sec → treat (unexpected early reward!)
- Wait 90 sec → treat
- Wait 180 sec → treat
- Wait 60 sec → treat
- Wait 150 sec → treat

This keeps Remi engaged without knowing when the next treat comes, creating a "slot machine" effect that maintains high motivation.

## Key Indicators to Watch

### ✅ Signs Remi is Ready to Advance
- Settles quickly after "get under"
- Minimal scanning/alertness during interval
- Relaxed body posture (loose muscles, possible eye contact with you)
- Breathing is calm and steady
- No whining, barking, or pawing

### ⚠️ Signs to Slow Down or Retreat
- Increased vigilance (ears forward, body tense)
- Repetitive behaviors (licking, pacing under chair)
- Trying to leave the under-table area
- Whale eye (showing whites of eyes)
- Whale/pant at rest
- Any vocalization

### 🔄 Regression Protocol
If Remi shows signs of stress or regression:
1. **Immediately** return to previous successful interval
2. Add a higher-value treat for successful calm
3. Reduce session length by 25%
4. Practice in a quieter environment before returning to challenging location

### New Restaurants (Medium Distraction)

- Focus on reliability over duration

## Troubleshooting Common Issues

### Problem: Remi gets up when treats stop
**Solution:** He's learned to associate getting up with session end. Practice "release" commands even when no treat is given. Say "okay!" and have him switch positions before ending session.

### Problem: Remi becomes fixated on handler
**Solution:** This is good engagement but may indicate over-reliance. Practice with occasional "life rewards" — letting him sniff, move, etc. between intervals.

### Problem: Success at home but fails in public
**Solution:** Increase distraction training separately. Start with shorter intervals in new environments and build back up.

### Problem: Remi seems "bored" or disengaged
**Solution:** Use variable reward values. Sometimes a small treat, sometimes a higher-value treat. Occasionally pair with verbal praise or a quick pet.
