# Survey of Existing Open-Source Humanoid Robot Projects

## Executive Summary

This document provides a comprehensive survey of existing open-source humanoid robot projects that are available for makers, researchers, and hobbyists. The research identifies four major open-source platforms—Poppy Humanoid, Berkeley Humanoid Lite, pib, and InMoov—along with their specifications, costs, and capabilities. Each project offers distinct advantages depending on the user's goals, budget, and technical sophistication. The landscape of open-source humanoid robotics has matured significantly, with recent projects like Berkeley Humanoid Lite achieving sub-$5,000 price points while maintaining research-quality capabilities.

## 1. Overview of Open-Source Humanoid Robotics

Open-source humanoid robot projects have evolved considerably over the past decade, transitioning from largely educational platforms to legitimate research-grade systems. The ecosystem now spans a wide spectrum—from simple robotic arm platforms to full bipedal systems with dozens of degrees of freedom.

### 1.1 Current State of the Field

The open-source humanoid robotics landscape in 2024-2025 shows significant diversity in design philosophy and target use cases:

| Category | Examples | Primary Use Case |
|----------|----------|------------------|
| Research-grade | Berkeley Humanoid Lite, Poppy Humanoid | Academic research, RL training |
| Educational | pib, Poppy Humanoid | STEM education, learning robotics |
| Maker/Hobbyist | InMoov, custom builds | Personal projects, prototyping |
| Development Platforms | Various ROS-based systems | Software development, manipulation research |

The field has seen particular advancement in **sim-to-real transfer**, with projects like Humanoid-Gym demonstrating zero-shot deployment of reinforcement learning policies from simulation to real hardware.

## 2. Major Open-Source Projects

### 2.1 Berkeley Humanoid Lite

**Overview:** Berkeley Humanoid Lite is the most recent and arguably the most capable open-source humanoid robot platform currently available. Developed by the Hybrid Robotics Lab at UC Berkeley, this project represents a significant achievement in making research-grade humanoid robotics accessible.

**Key Specifications:**

- **Cost:** Under $5,000 (complete)
- **Degrees of Freedom:** 20 DOF (configurable)
- **Actuators:** Custom 3D-printed modular gearboxes with brushless DC motors
- **Height:** Approximately 60cm (24 inches)
- **Weight:** ~7kg (15 lbs)
- **Control Framework:** Isaac Lab, ROS2 compatible
- **Documentation:** Extensive (GitBook, paper, video)

**Strengths:**
- Extremely affordable for a bipedal humanoid
- Modern software stack with Isaac Lab integration
- Excellent documentation and community support
- Designed for reinforcement learning research
- Custom gearboxes allow modification

**Weaknesses:**
- Requires significant assembly effort
- Custom components may be difficult to source
- Smaller stature limits some applications

**Resources:**
- [GitHub Repository](https://github.com/HybridRobotics/Berkeley-Humanoid-Lite)
- [Documentation](https://berkeley-humanoid-lite.gitbook.io/berkeley-humanoid-lite-docs)
- [Research Paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.17249)
- [Website](https://lite.berkeley-humanoid.org/)

### 2.2 Poppy Humanoid

**Overview:** Poppy Humanoid is one of the oldest and most established open-source humanoid platforms, developed by the Flowers Laboratory at Inria Bordeaux. It has a strong history in research and education, with particular emphasis on embodiment and morphological computation.

**Key Specifications:**

- **Cost:** $8,000-$9,000 (actuators represent ~60% of cost)
- **Degrees of Freedom:** 25 DOF
- **Actuators:** Robotis Dynamixel series (MX-64, MX-28, AX-12+)
- **Height:** 84cm (33 inches)
- **Weight:** ~3.5kg (without sensors)
- **Control Framework:** Raspberry Pi 3/4, Python, ROS
- **Licenses:** Hardware (CC BY-SA), Software (GPL v3)

**Strengths:**
- Mature, well-documented platform
- Uses industry-standard Dynamixel servos
- Strong community and forum support
- Educational materials available
- Modular design

**Weaknesses:**
- Higher cost due to premium actuators
- Older software stack (Python 2 heritage, though updated)
- No native leg actuation (stand-only, not mobile)

**Resources:**
- [GitHub Repository](https://github.com/poppy-project/poppy-humanoid)
- [Website](https://www.poppy-project.org/)
- [Documentation](https://docs.poppy-project.org/)
- [Forum](https://forum.poppy-project.org/)

### 2.3 pib (Printable Intelligent Bot)

**Overview:** pib is a German open-source project that emphasizes accessibility for makers and educational purposes. It is designed to be printed on consumer 3D printers and assembled with minimal tools.

**Key Specifications:**

- **Cost:** Approximately $1,500-$2,500 (depends on servo selection)
- **Degrees of Freedom:** 17 DOF (standard configuration)
- **Actuators:** Varies (MG996R common, but upgradable)
- **Height:** ~45cm (18 inches)
- **Weight:** ~2kg
- **Control Framework:** Arduino/Raspberry Pi, custom software
- **Community:** Discord, active development

**Strengths:**
- Very affordable
- Designed for home 3D printing
- Good educational resources
- Active community
- Multiple configurations available

**Weaknesses:**
- Lower payload and precision than premium options
- Limited documentation compared to academic projects
- Simpler control systems

**Resources:**
- [Website](https://pib.rocks/)
- [Shop](https://shop.pib.rocks)
- [Discord Community](https://discord.gg/GRdpyeDu7P)

### 2.4 InMoov

**Overview:** InMoov is the pioneer of open-source life-size humanoid robots, created by French designer Gaël Langevin beginning in 2012. It is unique in that it was originally started as an open-source prosthetic hand project and expanded into a full humanoid platform.

**Key Specifications:**

- **Cost:** $1,500-$3,000 (highly variable)
- **Degrees of Freedom:** Configurable (typically 20+ for full version)
- **Actuators:** MG996R, MG90S, or Dynamixel (user choice)
- **Height:** ~180cm (life-size)
- **Weight:** ~25kg (varies with configuration)
- **Control Framework:** Arduino, Raspberry Pi, MyRobotLab
- **Community:** Large global community

**Strengths:**
- True life-size humanoid
- Vast documentation and community knowledge
- Extremely modular (print only what you need)
- Huge parts library on Thingiverse
- Low cost entry point

**Weaknesses:**
- Complex assembly (100+ hours for full build)
- No standardized BOM (highly user-dependent)
- Quality varies with servo selection
- Legs are typically non-motorized in many builds

**Resources:**
- [Website](https://inmoov.fr/)
- [Build Guides](https://inmoov.fr/build-yours/)
- [Parts Gallery](https://inmoov.fr/inmoov-stl-parts-viewer/)
- [Google Groups Forum](https://groups.google.com/forum/#!forum/inmoov)
- [Thingiverse](http://thingiverse.com/poppy_project/)

## 3. Actuator and Servo Options

The choice of actuators fundamentally determines the capabilities and cost of any humanoid robot project.

### 3.1 Premium Options (Research-Grade)

| Model | Torque | Weight | Price | Features |
|-------|-------|--------|-------|-----------|
| Robotis MX-64T | 6.0 N·m | 126g | ~$120 | Position feedback, daisy-chain |
| Robotis MX-28T | 2.5 N·m | 72g | ~$70 | Position feedback, daisy-chain |
| Robotis XL430-W250 | 1.5 N·m | 61g | ~$60 | Budget smart servo |
| Robotis XH540-V270 | 7.3 N·m | 173g | ~$150 | High-torque option |
| Robotis RH-P12-RN | 12.5 N·m | 485g | ~$350 | Extra heavy-duty |

**Recommendation:** For Poppy Humanoid or similar projects, Robotis Dynamixel servos are the industry standard. They provide position feedback, allow daisy-chaining, and have robust software libraries.

### 3.2 Budget Options (Maker-Grade)

| Model | Torque | Weight | Price | Features |
|-------|-------|--------|-------|-----------|
| MG996R | 11 kg·cm | 55g | ~$10 | Metal gear, no feedback |
| MG90S | 2.2 kg·cm | 17g | ~$8 | Metal gear, small form |
| SG90 | 2.5 kg·cm | 9g | ~$5 | Plastic gear, micro |
| DS3225 | 25 kg·cm | 58g | ~$20 | Digital alternative |

**Note:** Standard hobby servos (MG996R, SG90) lack position feedback, making closed-loop control difficult. Modifications exist to add feedback, or consider digital servos with feedback capabilities.

### 3.3 Custom Gearbox Approach

For Berkeley Humanoid Lite and similar advanced builds, custom 3D-printed gearboxes with brushless DC motors offer the best price-to-performance ratio. This approach:
- Requires machining custom parts (or high-quality 3D printing with gear-grade filaments)
- Needs gear reduction systems (planetary, harmonic)
- Involves electronics separate from actuators
- Provides highest customization potential

## 4. Control Frameworks

### 4.1 ROS2 (Robot Operating System 2)

ROS2 is the current standard for research robotics, offering:

- **Modular architecture** with nodes for sensors, control, perception
- **rviz2** for visualization
- **Gazebo** for simulation (or Isaac Sim)
- **MoveIt2** for motion planning
- **rclpy/rclcpp** for Python/C++ interface

For Poppy Humanoid, there is an **extended repository** integrating with ROS.

### 4.2 Isaac Lab

Berkeley Humanoid Lite uses **Isaac Lab** (NVIDIA's robot learning framework), which provides:
- GPU-accelerated simulation
- Built-in reinforcement learning pipelines
- PhysX-based physics
- Domain randomization tools
- Ideal for sim2real workflows

### 4.3 MyRobotLab

InMoov uses **MyRobotLab**, a Python-based control framework:
- Cross-platform (Windows, Linux, Mac)
- Easy Arduino integration
- Voice control capabilities
- Mobile phone遥控 support
- Less formal but capable

### 4.4 Custom/Standalone

For simpler projects:
- Arduino with Servo library
- Raspberry Pi with Python GPIO
- ESP32-based distributed control
- BeagleBone Black with PRU firmware

## 5. AI/ML Integration for Autonomous Behavior

### 5.1 Reinforcement Learning Frameworks

| Framework | Best For | Repository |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| Humanoid-Gym | Sim2Real transfer | [roboterax/humanoid-gym](https://github.com/roboterax/humanoid-gym) |
| Isaac Lab | GPU-accelerated RL | NVIDIA Isaac |
| RSL-RL | Legged robot training | [legged gym](https://github.com/leggedrobotics/legged_gym) |
| OWL-ViT | Object manipulation | Various |

### 5.2 Sim2Real Workflows

The standard approach for learning autonomous bipedal locomotion:

1. **Simulation Training:** Train policies in Gazebo, Isaac Lab, or MuJoCo
2. **Domain Randomization:** Vary friction, mass, latency, lighting
3. **Zero-Shot Transfer:** Deploy trained policies directly on hardware
4. **Real-World Fine-Tuning:** Optional adaptation

### 5.3 Perception and Navigation

- **SLAM:** Cartographer, RTAB-Map, ORB-SLAM3
- **Object Detection:** YOLO, DetectNet, RGB-D fusion
- **Navigation:** Nav2 (ROS2), navigation2 stack
- **LLM Integration:** For high-level task planning and natural language understanding

## 6. 3D Printing Requirements

### 6.1 Printer Specifications

For all major open-source projects:

- **Minimum Build Volume:** 120×120×120mm (for InMoov parts)
- **Recommended Build Volume:** 200×200×200mm or larger
- **Layer Resolution:** 0.1-0.2mm typical

| Project | Printer Requirements | Recommended Size |
|----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Berkeley Humanoid Lite | Medium (gearbox parts) | 200mm+ |
| Poppy Humanoid | Medium (full body) | 200mm+ |
| pib | Small-Medium | 180mm+ |
| InMoov | Small (modular assembly) | 120mm+ |

### 6.2 Filament Recommendations

| Filament | Use Case | Pros | Cons |
|----------|----------|------|------|
| PLA | Prototyping, non-stressed parts | Easy to print, cheap | Low heat resistance, brittle |
| PLA+ | General parts | Better durability | Slightly harder to print |
| PETG | Moving parts, structural | Strong, water-resistant | Requires more heat |
| PETG-CF | High-stress components | Carbon fiber reinforced | Abrasive to nozzles |
| Nylon | gears, high-wear parts | Extremely durable | Requires enclosure |
| TPU | Grips, flexible parts | Very flexible | Slow to print |

**Recommendation:** Use PETG for structural components and moving parts. Use TPU for grippers and flexible elements.

### 6.3 Hardware Requirements Checklist

For humanoid robot assembly, you'll need:

- **3D Printer:** FDM with minimum 180×180×180mm build volume
- **Soldering Equipment:** For servo wire modifications
- **Screwdrivers:** Precision set (Pentalobe, Phillips)
- **Wrenches:** Metric set for servo horn screws
- **Wire Strippers:** 20-30 AWG
- **Multimeter:** For continuity testing
- **Heat Sink/Enclosure:** For Raspberry Pi (if using)
- **Power Supply:** 12V 5A or higher (project-dependent)
